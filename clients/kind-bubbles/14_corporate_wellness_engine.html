01
The Wellness Lead
Tech HQs · biotech · finance · wellness-coded employers
Likely titles
Director of People Experience · Head of Wellness · Workplace Experience Manager · Benefits & Wellbeing Lead · Total Rewards Manager · DEI + Belonging Lead
What they care about
Employees forgetting to look at their phones for 60 minutes. They're constantly searching for "wellness experiences that aren't another lunch yoga class." Quantifiable engagement (photos, attendance, post-event survey). Vendor reliability. Most underrated: they need something they can put on the company Slack that isn't a meditation app link.
Budget posture
Per-event vendor budgets typically sit in the same range as a quarterly catered lunch or a guest-speaker honorarium — set with the buyer directly when you quote. Annual wellness contracts (4–8 events / year) are common at mid-sized companies; larger companies sometimes bundle 6–12 vendor experiences into a single annual line item. Specific dollar ranges set in Phase 2 once Chris has 2–3 corporate events to anchor pricing on.
What stops the deal
Liability paperwork (need clean COI), time-of-day fit (most book 12pm–2pm or 4pm–6pm), vendor PO requirements, and the need for one Slack-friendly visual asset post-event. Solve all four upfront.

02
The School Decision-Maker
Private + public schools · enrichment + SEL programs
Likely titles
Head of Lower School · Director of Student Life · Director of Wellness or Counseling · PTA / Parent Council Chair · Specialist Teachers (PE, science, art)
What they care about
SEL alignment, science-of-learning credibility, and a memory the kids will talk about at home. Everything they buy gets reviewed by a parent committee — the program needs to make the parent committee proud, not just entertain the kids.
Budget posture
School assembly budgets vary widely with school size + duration; multi-visit residencies (3–6 visits over a semester) are sold as a package. PTA and Parent Council discretionary funds often move faster than district-level approvals. Set specific pricing tiers in Phase 2 with 1–2 anchor school bookings.
What stops the deal
Lack of a one-page program brief that links the experience to learning outcomes. Insurance + COI. Background-check confirmation. Schedule fit (most school programs book Sept–Dec for spring; Jan–Mar for fall). Have all four ready before first outreach.
First-touch email

Subject: A science-of-wonder assembly for [School name]

03
The Hospitality Buyer
Boutique hotels · resorts · members' clubs · short-term rentals
Likely titles
Director of Member Experience · F&B and Programming Lead · Director of Recreation · Family Programs Manager · Front Office GM
What they care about
Memorable signature programming guests will Instagram about. Hospitality is the lane where the founder narrative + science angle + sensory experience compounds — guests pay for a hotel that feels different. Especially valuable: family-friendly resort programming, summer-pool kid activations, and members' club kid weekends.
Budget
— per activation for a boutique hotel weekend. — per multi-event seasonal residency at a members' club or resort. Hospitality pays the most because the experience drives bookings, room nights, member retention.
What stops the deal
Lack of past hospitality client examples. Insurance + COI naming the property. Schedule confirmation 60+ days out. The one shortcut: any past hospitality engagement (even a single weekend) becomes the next 5 hospitality contracts' opening line.
First-touch email

Boutique hotels and member clubs in the Bay Area book us for seasonal residencies. I'd love to send you a one-pager on what a Saturday at [Property name] could look like — or if you've got a specific season in mind, I can put together a quick concept.

The annual-contract conversion

One event becomes
four events.

Single events are the warm-up. The real B2B value is the annual contract — and most buyers will agree to it after one good event because it's easier for them to plan, easier to budget, and easier to defend internally. Always position the annual at the moment of closing the first event.

What your strategy partner owns

You show up to the gigs.
The funnel runs around you.

The B2B sales engine has more moving parts than the consumer side — outreach, list management, follow-ups, deck customization, contract handling. This is where having a content + brand strategy partner saves the most time. The founder shows up where the founder voice matters (kickoff calls, on-site events, strategic accounts). Everything else runs in the background:

Strategy partner takes off your plate
  1. Lead list management — Apify-enriched contact lists per persona, refreshed monthly with new openings.
  2. First-touch outreach cadence — sending the first email, second touch, third touch. Founder responds when prospects reply.
  3. Deck customization — per-prospect mini decks built from a master template (one per persona).
  4. COI + contract logistics — single proof-of-insurance routed per event, contract templates kept fresh.
  5. Post-event content — recap photos, founder recap email, social proof for the next pitch.
  6. Quarterly pipeline review — what's working, what's not, where to push next.
The founder voice is what closes corporate deals — but only if the founder has time to actually show up to the first kickoff calls. The partner's job is to keep that calendar uncluttered and the prospects warm.
