When it is useful

Three law-firm moments where public signals matter.

The read is strongest when something already moved: a partner left, a competitor hired, a new office opened, or a practice lane started getting crowded.

A

You got raided

A competitor took a partner, team, or practice leader. The question is whether that creates a staffing issue, a client issue, or a market-narrative issue worth answering.

B

You are doing the raiding

You spent on laterals. The question is whether the market can see what you are building, or whether the hire is still trapped inside a generic announcement.

C

A rival is building your lane

You may still own the capability, but competitors are buying the public build story. The question is whether to defend, flank, amplify, or ignore it.

Decision menu

Law-firm market reads work when there is an expensive decision on the desk. The output is not a data dump. It is a recommendation about what to own next.

  • Which practice area should we push next?
  • Are competitors building a lane we need to defend?
  • Did a lateral hire signal a market move or just generic growth?
  • Should we package a fixed-fee front door or defend premium hourly work?
  • Are we targeting the end client or the referral partner?
  • Which competitors are spending into our market, and what are they testing?
  • What buyer moment should we own before the market gets crowded?
  • Should we invest in SEO, ads, social, partner outreach, or nothing yet?

Sample read

The useful feeling is: "I knew some of that, but not all of it."

A strong read respects what the firm already knows, then shows what the market is doing around them: named competitors, visible spend, hiring movement, buyer language, and the implication.

Example: managed-care lane defense

  1. Known: One rival is visibly adding health care partners across several offices.
  2. Competitor movement: Other firms are also adding managed-care, payor, Medicaid, fraud/abuse, and regulatory bench in the same orbit.
  3. Talent signal: The named attorneys matter less as isolated hires and more as a map of which lane firms think is worth buying.
  4. Positioning signal: The target firm may still have the stronger capability, but competitors are compounding the public build story.
  5. Decision: Defend the managed-care/payor lane now, or let lateral buyers become category leaders by repetition.

Most firms see the pieces separately. We connect them before you commit the budget.

Guardrails

This is market intelligence, not legal advice.

What data do you use?

Public data only: competitor sites, ads, blogs, social, attorney announcements, job postings, search language, reviews, local news, agency releases, trade groups, and visible referral-market activity.

Is this SEO research?

No. SEO is one channel. We look across the market and turn the signals into a decision: what the firm should own next, what to ignore, and where the current positioning is weak.

Is this recruiting?

No. We do not source candidates. We use public lateral movement as a market signal: who is buying which capability, what lane that points to, and whether the firm should defend, flank, amplify, or ignore it.

Can you predict who leaves next?

No. We map public movement and show where risk or opportunity appears concentrated. That is pattern work, not certainty, and the read stays inside public-source evidence.

What if the answer is "do nothing"?

Then that is the answer. A good read should sometimes stop a firm from spending on another page, campaign, hire, or content push.
